# Brainstorming

Treat this as an open conversation first. The goal is to surface everything worth saying, then organize it into a shape.

## Two-stage rhythm

**Stage 1 – Collect.** Ask questions, explore angles, push on what's genuinely interesting vs what's been said before. Good questions:
- What's the core insight? What does John know that most people get wrong or don't see yet?
- Who is this for, specifically? What does that reader need to walk away with?
- What's the "moment of surprise" – the thing that reframes how they think?
- What's out of scope for this post (but might be a follow-up)?

Don't rush to structure yet. Let ideas pile up.

**Stage 2 – Organize.** Once the ideas are out, help shape the **vehicle** – the narrative frame that carries the ideas most clearly. Examples: a timeline, a before/after, a problem–solution, a guided demo, a series of contrasts, a single extended analogy. The vehicle determines section order and what earns what position.

## Saving the work

Once there's enough to refer back to, save a brainstorming notes file:

```
work-in-progress/<post-slug>/brainstorm.md
```

Use a short, meaningful slug (e.g. `rag-latency-tradeoffs`, `agentic-loops-in-prod`). Create the directory if it doesn't exist. Keep saving incrementally as the conversation develops – don't wait until the end.

## Quality checks toward the end

As the brainstorm matures, draw attention to these:

- **Actionable for readers?** Can they do something differently after reading this? If it's pure insight with no action, is that a conscious choice?
- **Hook?** Is there a lead-in moment that makes a technically literate reader want to keep reading? Something surprising, counterintuitive, or that names a real pain?
- **Takeaway lessons?** Can we articulate 2–3 things the reader will walk away knowing or thinking differently about?

Once the ideas and rough structure are clear, offer a **candidate title** – see `references/reviewing-outline.md` for title guidance.
